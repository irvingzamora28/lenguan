# Lesson 5: Basic Grammar - Verbs and Sentence Structure in the Present Tense

## Introduction

Building on what we've learned about nouns and pronouns, it's time to dive into verbs and sentence structures. In this lesson, we focus on the present tense, which describes current actions or general truths. Understanding verb conjugation and sentence construction is essential for effective communication.

## Section 1: Verb Conjugation in the Present Tense

Verbs in German change their endings according to the subject of the sentence. Here's how to conjugate regular verbs in the present tense:

### Regular Verb Conjugation Pattern

| Pronoun   | Ending | Beispiel (Example)          |
| --------- | ------ | --------------------------- |
| ich       | -e     | ich mache (I do/make)       |
| du        | -st    | du machst (you do/make)     |
| er/sie/es | -t     | er macht (he does/makes)    |
| wir       | -en    | wir machen (we do/make)     |
| ihr       | -t     | ihr macht (you all do/make) |
| Sie/sie   | -en    | Sie machen (you do/make)    |

## Section 2: Sentence Structure

In German, the basic sentence structure is Subject-Verb-Object (SVO), just like in English. However, the verb always takes the second position in the sentence.

| Subject | Verb    | Object     | Complete Sentence  |
| ------- | ------- | ---------- | ------------------ |
| Ich     | lese    | ein Buch   | Ich lese ein Buch. |
| Du      | trinkst | Wasser     | Du trinkst Wasser. |
| Er      | hat     | einen Hund | Er hat einen Hund. |

<TipBox>
    - **Verb Position**: In a German sentence, the verb is usually the second element in the statement. Even if the sentence starts with something other than the subject, the verb still remains in the second position.
    <br> For example: "Heute gehe ich ins Kino." (Today I go to the cinema.) Despite "Heute" leading the sentence, "gehe" remains the second element.
    - **Compound Sentences**: When connecting sentences with conjunctions like "und" (and) or "aber" (but), the verb in the following clause also takes the second position.
    <br> For example: "Ich spiele Tennis und sie sieht zu." (I play tennis and she watches.)
    - **Question Structure**: When forming a question without a question word, the verb comes first.
    <br> For example: "Kommst du mit?" (Are you coming along?)
    - **Time Manner Place (TMP) Rule**: When constructing a sentence, follow the TMP rule for the order of adverbial phrases: Time first, then manner, and place last.
    <br> For example: "Ich gehe heute Nachmittag zu Fuß in den Park." (I am going on foot to the park this afternoon.)
</TipBox>
